Mutation - Consequences of Mitotic Errors

Background Information:

Source Source

During mitosis, errors in chromosome segregation can cause significant DNA damage. This damage can lead to structural alterations in chromosomes, and cause diseases like cancer.

Recent evidence suggests that errors in chromosome segregation, like lagging chromosomes or SAC (Spindle assembly checkpoint) defects, can lead to:

  • Aneuploidy - a condition where cells have an abnormal number of chromosomes.
  • Chromothripsis - a phenomenon where chromosomes shatter and reassemble, resulting in highly localized chromosomal rearrangements.
  • Micronuclei - fragmented chromosomes that fail to integrate into the main cell nucleus and become trapped in small nuclei that are fragile and prone to DNA damage.

Errors in mitosis and chromosomal instability (CIN) can lead to cancer progression, especially when cells acquire oncogenes or experience chromosomal rearrangements.

Question 2: hBUB1 Mutations in Lung Cancer

Recent studies have shown that mutations in the hBUB1 gene are linked to lung cancer. In one study, somatic mutations were found in lung cancer cell lines and primary tumors. The following mutations were detected in the hBUB1 gene:

  • A cytosine-to-guanine substitution in codon 51 of exon 5, leading to a histidine-to-aspartic acid amino acid substitution.
  • A thymine-to-cytosine substitution in codon 430 of exon 12, which did not result in an amino acid substitution.
  • A guanine-to-thymine substitution in codon 93 of exon 4, found in both a lung cancer cell line and a primary tumor.

What is the effect of a somatic mutation in the hBUB1 gene in the context of lung cancer?









Correct!

  • Lagging chromosomes acquire DNA damage and may be partitioned into micronuclei.
  • hBUB1 mutations result in chromosomal instability, contributing to tumor progression and aneuploidy.
