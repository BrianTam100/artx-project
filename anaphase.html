Anaphase - Cell Division Process

Source

During Anaphase, the sister chromatids of each chromosome are separated and pulled toward opposite poles of the cell. This ensures that each daughter cell will receive a complete set of chromosomes. The separation is facilitated by microtubules that attach to the kinetochores of each chromatid and pull them apart.

Possible complications during anaphase: Nondisjunction and Aneuploidy

Nondisjunction is the failure of chromosomes or chromatids to separate properly during cell division. When this occurs, it results in daughter cells with an abnormal number of chromosomes, leading to a condition known as aneuploidy.

Types of Aneuploidy

Aneuploidy can result in the following:

  • Monosomy (n-1): One chromosome is missing. Example: Turner Syndrome (45, X).
  • Trisomy (n+1): An extra chromosome is present. Example: Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21).

Nondisjunction

Meiotic nondisjunction, which occurs during the formation of gametes, can lead to:

  • Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21): This results in an intellectual disability and increased risk of certain health conditions.
  • Patau Syndrome (Trisomy 13): Associated with severe developmental and physical abnormalities, with a life expectancy typically less than one year.
  • Edwards Syndrome (Trisomy 18): Associated with severe intellectual disabilities, heart defects, and early mortality.
